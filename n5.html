Chapter 1 — What Is Joylang?

Joylang is a constructed auxiliary language — an invented language designed so that speakers of very different languages can communicate easily, warmly, and playfully. It is not a replacement for your native language; it is a bridge.

Imagine a group of friends from India, Japan, Spain, Nigeria, Turkey, and Brazil. Each has a rich language. Each has spent years mastering it. But when they meet, none of them has a shared tongue that is equally easy and equally foreign for everyone. Joylang is designed to solve that problem. It is nobody's native language — which means the playing field is level for everyone.

Three Core Values

ValueWhat It Means in Practice
SimplicityNo irregular verbs. No grammatical gender. No tones. No exceptions. Every rule applies 100% of the time.
PlayfulnessWords are chosen to sound a little delightful. Grammar is gentle, not military. Making mistakes is encouraged.
Universal AccessibilitySounds exist in Hindi, Arabic, Japanese, Spanish, Swahili, Tamil, Turkish, English, Mandarin, and Yoruba. Everyone can pronounce every word.

Quick Facts

FeatureJoylang's Answer
Word orderSOV — Subject, Object, Verb (like Japanese, Turkish, Hindi)
MorphologyLightly agglutinative — words built by adding suffixes
Grammatical genderNone
TonesNone
ScriptRoman (this course) + Devanagari (alternative)
Vocabulary sourcesHindi, Arabic, Mandarin, Japanese, Swahili, Spanish, Tamil, Turkish, English, Yoruba
Phoneme inventory5 vowels + 19 consonants (including 3 digraphs: sh, ch, ng)
Irregular formsZero

Chapter 2 — Sounds & Pronunciation

Joylang uses only sounds that exist in the most widely spoken languages on Earth. If you speak any of the 10 source languages, you can already make every sound in Joylang.

The 5 Vowels

Joylang has exactly 5 vowels. They are always pronounced the same way — there are no 'silent' vowels and no vowel shifts.

VowelSounds LikeEnglish HintExample in Joylang

Vowel Rule Every vowel is always pronounced clearly. There are no schwa sounds (like the 'a' in 'about'). What you see is what you say.

Chapter 3 — Writing Joylang

Joylang uses Roman letters as its primary writing system. The spelling is perfectly phonetic — every letter always represents exactly one sound, and every sound is always represented by the same letter(s).

Phonetic Writing Rule What you see is what you say. What you hear is what you write. No exceptions.

Word Boundaries — Suffix Attachment

In Joylang, suffixes attach directly to roots without spaces. A case suffix, tense suffix, or class suffix is always written as part of the word:

sato-ombu → satoombu → friend (noun form)
kachi-lo → kachilo → eats (present tense)
mavombu-ko → mavombuko → water (accusative)
Hyphens are used in teaching materials to show boundaries. In normal writing, no hyphen is used.

Same-Vowel Elision

When a root ends in a vowel and a suffix begins with the same vowel, drop the root's final vowel before attaching.

RootSuffixWithout ElisionWith Elision (correct)
pani-ichupaniichupanichu ✓
sato-ongusatoongusatongu ✓
mavi-ichumaviichumavichu ✓
⚠ Common Mistake Elision only happens when the SAME vowel appears at the boundary. Different vowels do NOT elide: mavi + ombu → mav + ombu = mavombu (i≠o, no elision — stem rule applies).

Chapter 5 — Basic Sentence Structure (SOV)

SOV Word Order Subject → Object → Verb
Anu maviombu kachilo. = I water eat. = "I eat water."

SOV is the most common word order among human languages (used by about 45% of all languages), including Japanese, Turkish, Hindi, and Korean.

Simple Sentences (No Objects)

Anu velo. → I go.
Venu belo. → He/she comes.
Anuchi nalulo. → We sleep.
Tenu shojulo. → You laugh.

Sentences With Objects

Anu sato doshilo. → I see a friend.
Venu maviombu shapilo. → He/she drinks water.

Present Tense — add -lo to verb root

RootMeaning+ -loPresent Meaning
kachieatkachiloeats / is eating
shojulaughshojulolaughs
nalusleepnalulosleeps
velogovelogoes (already ends in -lo)
belocomebelocomes (already ends in -lo)
melobe/existmelois/exists (copula — used as-is)

Adjective Sentences

Adjective Sentence Rule Subject + Adjective(-oku) + melo.
The adjective comes directly before melo.
Oshi goloku melo. → That is good.
Anu jovioku melo. → I am happy.
Sato boloku melo. → The friend is big/great.
Mavi shitoku melo. → The water is cold.

Chapter 6 — Saying No: The ve- Prefix

Negation in Joylang is expressed by a single prefix: ve-. It always attaches to the beginning of the verb. There are no double negatives, no irregular negative forms, and no changes based on tense or person.

The ve- Rule Positive: Subject Object Verb-tense.
Negative: Subject Object ve-Verb-tense.
Anu kachilo. → I eat.
Anu vekachilo. → I do not eat.

ve- With Different Tenses

TensePositiveNegative
PresentAnu kachilo.Anu vekachilo.
PastAnu kachime.Anu vekachime.
FutureAnu kachibu.Anu vekachibu.
ProgressiveAnu kachishilo.Anu vekachishilo.

Negating 'to be' Sentences

Oshi goloku melo. → That is good.
Oshi goloku vemelo. → That is not good.
Anu shishyombu melo. → I am a student.
Anu shishyombu vemelo. → I am not a student.

Emphatic Negation

To add extra emphasis, add kadanu (never/absolutely not) after the verb:

Anu vekachilo kadanu. → I absolutely do not eat. / I never eat.
⚠ Common Mistake Never use ve- on a noun or adjective. Only the verb gets ve-.
✗ WRONG: Anu veshishyombu melo.
✓ RIGHT: Anu shishyombu vemelo.

✏ Practice — Chapter 6

  1. Make negative: 'Venu shojulo.' (She laughs.)
  2. Translate: 'We are not happy.' (use anuchi, jovioku)
  3. Which is correct? 'Anu vemaviombu kachilo.' OR 'Anu maviombu vekachilo.'
  4. Translate: 'I never sleep.' (nalu = sleep)
  1. Venu veshojulo.
  2. Anuchi jovioku vemelo.
  3. 'Anu maviombu vekachilo.' is correct (ve- on the verb, not the noun).
  4. Anu nalulo kadanu. (kadanu = never)

Chapter 7 — Asking Questions: The ka Particle

Joylang forms questions by adding a single word: ka. It goes at the very beginning of the sentence, before everything else. The rest of the sentence order stays exactly the same.

The ka Question Rule Statement: Subject Object Verb.
Question: ka Subject Object Verb?

Yes/No Questions

StatementQuestion
Anu jovioku melo.Ka anu jovioku melo?
Venu shojulo.Ka venu shojulo?
Tenu nashilo.Ka tenu nashilo?

Answering Yes/No Questions

AnswerJoylangNotes
YesanuAffirmative response particle
NoveShort for 've melo'
MaybeshoruAlso means 'apparently'
I don't knowanu vejaniloLiterally: 'I not-know'

Content Questions (who, what, where…)

Ka kenu velo? → Who came?
Ka chenu eshi melo? → What is this?
Ka kanu tenu melo? → Where are you?
Ka manu tenu belo? → When will you come?
Ka yenu tenu vekachilo? → Why don't you eat?
Ka henu tenu nashilo? → How do you learn?

Tag Questions

To add a tag question (like 'right?' or 'isn't it?'), add sachianu? at the end:

Oshi goloku melo, sachianu? → That is good, right?
Tenu shishyombu melo, sachianu? → You are a student, aren't you?

✏ Practice — Chapter 7

  1. Turn into a question: 'Anuchi nashilo.' (We learn.)
  2. Translate: 'Where is the friend?' (use satoombu)
  3. Translate: 'Why did he laugh?' (shoju-me = laughed)
  4. How do you say 'yes' and 'no' in Joylang?
  1. Ka anuchi nashilo?
  2. Ka satoombu kanu melo?
  3. Ka yenu venu shojume?
  4. Yes = anu, No = ve

Chapter 8 — Word Classes: Making Nouns, Verbs, and Adjectives

This is one of Joylang's most powerful features. Every root is class-neutral — it is just a concept. To use a concept as a noun, a verb, or an adjective, you attach the appropriate suffix.

The Three Class Suffixes

The Stem Rule Noun: root stem + -ombu
Verb: root stem + -uvu (dictionary form)
Adjective: root stem + -oku
Stem = root with final vowel removed: sato → sat- · mavi → mav- · jovi → jov-

Verb Forms in Sentences

⚠ Verb Tense Rule Tense attaches to the ROOT, not to -uvu. The -uvu form is only for the dictionary.
✗ Wrong: satuvu-lo    ✓ Right: sato-lo (befriends, present tense)
FormConstructionExampleMeaning

Using All Three Classes in One Sentence

Anu ronombu doshilo. → I see love. (noun)
Anu ronolo. → I love. (verb, present tense)
Venu ronoku melo. → He/she is loving. (adjective)
