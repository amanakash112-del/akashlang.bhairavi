Chapter 9 — The Case System: Who Does What To Whom

Cases tell you what role each noun plays in the sentence: who is acting, who receives the action, who owns what, and so on. In Joylang, cases are marked by small suffixes attached directly to the noun.

The 7 Cases

CaseSuffixRoleQuestion It Answers
Nominative-saSubject (who does the action)Who does it?
Accusative-koDirect object (receives action)What/whom?
Dative-raIndirect object (given to, for)To whom / for whom?
Genitive-niPossession / belongingWhose? / Of what?
Locative-meLocation or time (in, at, on)Where? When?
Ablative-taSource / starting point (from)From where?
Vocative-eDirect address (calling someone)O ___! / Hey ___!
Nominative Note The subject (nominative -sa) is usually omitted when the subject is a pronoun. Pronouns rarely take -sa in natural speech.
Anu kachilo. (not: anu-sa kachilo)
But: Satoombu-sa kachilo. = The friend eats. (noun subject takes -sa)

Case Examples

Anu satoombu-ko doshilo.
I see a friend. (accusative -ko)
Anu satoombu-ra maviombu dilo.
I give water to the friend. (dative -ra)
Oshi sato-ni mavombu goloku melo.
That friend's water is good. (genitive -ni)
Anu vidyombu-me nashilo.
I learn at school. (locative -me)
Venu nagombu-ta belo.
He/she comes from the city. (ablative -ta)
Sato-e, ka tenu jovioku melo?
O friend, are you happy? (vocative -e)

Multiple Cases in One Sentence

Anu-sa satoombu-ra maviombu-ko dilo.
I give water to the friend.
Venu vidyombu-me shishyombu-ko doshilo.
At school, he/she sees a student.
⚠ Common Mistake Adjectives and adverbs NEVER take case suffixes. Only nouns and noun phrases take cases.
✗ WRONG: Anu goloku-ko melo. (putting case on an adjective)
✓ RIGHT: Anu goloku melo.

Chapter 10 — Tense: Past, Present, Future

Joylang marks time with suffixes attached directly to the verb root. There are exactly three tense suffixes and they never change.

TenseSuffixAttaches toExample (root: kachi)
Present-lorootkachilo — eats / is eating
Past-merootkachime — ate / was eating
Future-borootkachibu — will eat

All Three Tenses in Action

EnglishJoylang
I eat (now)Anu kachilo.
I ate (past)Anu kachime.
I will eat (future)Anu kachibu.
She sees a friend (now)Venu satoombu-ko doshilo.
She saw a friend (past)Venu satoombu-ko doshime.
She will see a friend (future)Venu satoombu-ko doshibu.
We came from the city (past)Anuchi nagombu-ta belome.
We will come from the cityAnuchi nagombu-ta belobu.

Tense With Negation

Anu vekachilo. → I don't eat.
Anu vekachime. → I didn't eat.
Anu vekachibu. → I will not eat.

Chapter 11 — Aspect: Ongoing and Completed Actions

Tense tells you WHEN (past/present/future). Aspect tells you HOW the action unfolds — is it still in progress? Has it been completed?

AspectMarkerMeaningConstruction
Progressive-shi-Action in progress / ongoingroot + -shi + tense suffix
Perfect-vi-Action completed, result still relevantroot + -vi + tense suffix
Aspect Construction Aspect markers go BETWEEN the root and the tense suffix:
root + -shi- + tense = progressive    root + -vi- + tense = perfect
kachi + shi + lo = kachishilo (is eating)
kachi + vi + me = kachivime (has/had eaten)

Progressive Aspect

TenseProgressive FormMeaning
Presentkachishilois eating (right now)
Pastkachishimewas eating
Futurekachishibuwill be eating
Anu mavombu-ko shapishilo. → I am drinking water. (right now)
Venu vidyombu-me nashishime. → She was studying at school.
Anuchi shojushilo! → We are laughing!

Perfect Aspect

TensePerfect FormMeaning
Present perfectkachivilohas eaten (recently, result visible now)
Past perfectkachivimehad eaten (before another past event)
Future perfectkachivibuwill have eaten (by a certain future time)
Anu kachivilo. Anu jovioku melo. → I have eaten. I am happy.
Venu velo bitianu kachivime. → Before going, she had eaten.

All Forms at a Glance

FormJoylangMeaning

Simple pastkachimeate
Simple futurekachibuwill eat
Progressive presentkachishilois eating
Progressive pastkachishimewas eating
Progressive futurekachishibuwill be eating

Chapter 12 — Deriving New Words

Beyond the three class suffixes (noun/verb/adj), Joylang has five derivational suffixes and reduplication that build more specific meanings from any root.

The Five Derivational Suffixes

SuffixMeaning It AddsExample (root: sato = friend)
-vachiagent / one who doessatovachi — friendship-maker
-chimuabstract noun (the concept)satochimu — the concept of friendship
-jikuadj from noun (having quality of)satojiku — friend-like / friendly
-ichudiminutive (small/dear)satichu — dear little friend
-onguaugmentative (big/intense)satongu — great friend / BFF

Full Derivation Example — root jovi (joy/happy)

FormJoylangMeaning
Rootjovijoy / happy (concept)
Nounjovombujoy, happiness
Verb (dict.)jovuvuto rejoice (citation form)
Adjectivejovokujoyful, happy
Agentjovivachione who brings joy / joy-bringer
Abstract nounjovichimuthe concept/nature of joy
Adj from nounjovijikujoy-related / pertaining to joy
Diminutivejovichulittle joy, a small delight
Augmentativejoviongugreat joy, immense happiness
Negated verbvejovilodoes not rejoice (present tense)
Plural nounjovombuchijoys (plural)
Reduplicationjojoviintensely joyful / habitually joyful

Reduplication — Habitual and Intense

Reduplication Rule To express a habitual action or intense state: repeat the first CV of the root before the root.
kachi → ka + kachi = kakachi (habitual eating / always eating)
For digraph-starting roots (sh, ch), take the first 3 chars: shoju → sho + shoju = shoshoju
RootReduplicatedMeaning
kachikakachihabitual eating / always eating
nalunanaludeeply asleep / chronic sleeper
jovijojoviextremely joyful
shojushoshojualways laughing
satosasatovery friend-like / constantly befriending

Chapter 13 — Postpositions & Location

English uses prepositions (before the noun). Joylang, as an SOV language, uses postpositions — words that come after the noun.

The 6 Core Postpositions

PostpositionMeaningUsed With CaseExample
boshifrom / starting fromablative -tanagombu-ta boshi belo → comes from the city
pelafor / on behalf of / pleasedative -rasato-ra pela → for the friend
nishinear / besidelocative -megrikombu nishi melo → is near home
tamauntil / up tolocative -mekalanu tama → until tomorrow
komiwith / together withnominativesato komi nashilo → learns with a friend
selawithout / lackingnominativesato sela nalulo → sleeps without a friend
Anu nagombu-ta boshi belo. → I come from the city.
Venu sato-ra pela kachilo. → He/she eats for the friend.
Anuchi grikombu nishi melo. → We are near home.
Anu sato sela nalulo. → I sleep without a friend (alone).

Chapter 14 — Evidentials: How Do You Know?

Evidentiality expresses the speaker's source of knowledge. In Joylang, it is optional — but using evidentials makes speech significantly more precise and trust-building.

The 4 Evidentials

EvidentialMeaningWhen to Use
diruI directly witnessed this / I saw it myselfPersonal observation
shoruI heard this / apparently (hearsay)Information from others
niruI infer this / it seems logicallyLogical deduction
boruI read / I know this from a sourceResearch, documents, books
Evidential Position Evidentials come at the END of the sentence, after the verb.
Sentence + evidential.
Anu kachilo diru. = I eat — I saw it (statement of direct knowledge)
Venu jovioku melo diru. → He/she is happy — I saw it myself.
Anuchi kalanu velo shoru. → We go tomorrow — apparently. (I heard)
Tenu nashishilo niru. → You seem to be studying — I infer.
Joylang goloku melo boru. → Joylang is good — I read it somewhere.
Venu shojume diru. → She laughed — I witnessed it.

Evidentials and Negation

Venu vekachilo niru. → He/she apparently doesn't eat. (inference)
Anu vejanilo boru. → I don't know — based on what I've read.
📌 Cultural Note Evidentials are one of the most culturally important features of Joylang. Using 'diru' vs 'shoru' vs 'niru' shows intellectual honesty — you are telling your listener exactly how reliable your information is. Joylang speakers value this transparency.

✏ Practice — Chapter 14

  1. Add an appropriate evidential: 'She is happy.' (you saw her smile)
  2. Add an appropriate evidential: 'They went home.' (your friend told you)
  3. Translate: 'He apparently doesn't like water.' (maviombu, vepriyoku)
  4. When would you use 'boru'? Give a real example.
  1. Venu jovioku melo diru. (diru = I saw it directly)
  2. Venu grikombu-ko belome shoru. (shoru = I heard it)
  3. Venu maviombu vepriyoku melo shoru.
  4. Use boru when you read something in a book, article, or reliable document. Example: Joylang boloku melo boru. — Joylang is great, I read it.
