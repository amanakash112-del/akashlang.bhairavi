All 18 universal grammar categories — how Joylang handles each one

1. Nouns — ✓ Fully Implemented

Every concept can become a noun using the -ombu suffix (stem + -ombu). Plurals use -chi. Seven cases mark grammatical role. No grammatical gender.

FeatureDecisionMechanism
GenderNONEAll nouns gender-neutral. Optional maru/firu/biru modifiers before pronouns.
PluralYES — optionalAdd -chi to noun: satoombu → satoombu-chi
CasesYES — 7 cases-sa -ko -ra -ni -me -ta -e
Articles (a/the)NONEContext determines definiteness. No 'a' or 'the' in Joylang.
Count vs massNot distinguishedSame noun form for 'water' and 'a water'
satoombu → friend / friendship (noun)
satoombu-chi → friends (plural)
sato-ni mavombu → the friend's water (genitive)
satoombu-ko doshilo → sees a friend (accusative)

2. Pronouns — ✓ Fully Implemented

Three pronouns: anu (I), tenu (you), venu (they/he/she). Plural by adding -chi. Reflexive by adding -shi. No case forms on pronouns (they take no case suffixes in natural speech).

PersonSingularPluralReflexive
1st (speaker)anu = I/meanuchi = we/usanushi = myself
2nd (listener)tenu = youtenuchi = you alltenushi = yourself
3rd (other)venu = he/she/theyvenuchi = they pl.venushi = themselves
📌 Pronoun Design Choice The pronoun 'venu' covers English he/she/they (singular). This eliminates the need to know gender to speak Joylang — a major accessibility win for speakers of gendered languages.

Gender specification (always optional): maru venu = he (male) · firu venu = she (female) · biru venu = they (non-binary)

3. Verbs — ✓ Fully Implemented

Verb roots take tense suffixes (-lo present, -me past, -bo future) and aspect markers (-shi- progressive, -vi- perfect). NO person agreement. NO number agreement. Every verb follows the same pattern.

FeatureDecisionExample
Person agreementNONEAnu kachilo = I eat. Venu kachilo = She eats. (same form)
Number agreementNONESame verb for singular and plural subjects.
Tense3 tenses-lo (present), -me (past), -bo (future)
Aspect2 aspects-shi- (progressive insert), -vi- (perfect insert)
Negationve- prefixvekachilo = does not eat
Irregular verbsZEROEvery verb follows the same suffix system

4. Adjectives — ✓ Fully Implemented

Adjectives use the -oku suffix. In predicative position (he is happy), they combine with melo (copula). In attributive position (happy friend), they precede the noun.

jovoku sato → happy friend (attributive)
sato jovoku melo → the friend is happy (predicative)
goloku mavi → good water
mavi goloku melo → the water is good

7. Determiners (Articles) — ✗ Intentionally Excluded

Joylang has NO articles (a, an, the). Many major world languages lack articles entirely (Hindi, Russian, Japanese, Chinese, Swahili). Demonstratives (eshi/oshi) cover specific pointing when needed.

EnglishJoylang EquivalentHow It Works
a friendsatoombuNo article — context implies 'a'
the friendoshi satoombuUse 'oshi' (that/the specific one) when definiteness matters
this friendeshi satoombuDemonstrative serves as determiner
every friendsatoombu-chiPlural often implies 'every' or 'all'

9. Conjunctions — ✓ Now Fully Documented

Coordinating Conjunctions

JoylangEnglishUseExample
chiandConnects equal elementsAnu chi tenu nashilo. — You and I learn.
ovaorPresents alternativesKa tenu kachilo ova nalulo? — Are you eating or sleeping?
tevabutShows contrastAnu jovioku melo teva tenu dukhoku melo. — I am happy but you are sad.

Subordinating Conjunctions

JoylangEnglishUseExample
yenubecause / whyCause-effectAnu nashilo yenu anu samilo mansilo. — I study because I want to understand.
nashi…tashiif…thenConditional clausesNashi tenu belo, tashi anu jovioku melobo. — If you come, then I will be happy.
bevanialthoughConcessive contrastBevani deroku melo, venu sachishilo. — Although it is far, he/she is working.
domaniwhileSimultaneous eventsAnu nashilo domani tenu shojulo. — I learn while you laugh.
loshiso thatPurpose clausesPela shapibo loshi anu samilo. — Please speak so that I understand.
tokamihoweverAdversative connectiveOshi goloku melo tokami dosoku boru. — That is good, however it is reportedly bad.
nashiveunlessNegative conditionNashibo nashive tenu janilo. — I will not go unless you know.
Word Order with Conjunctions Coordinating conjunctions (chi, ova, teva) go BETWEEN the elements they connect.
Subordinating conjunctions come at the START of the subordinate clause.
'nashi…tashi' is always a pair: nashi introduces the condition, tashi introduces the result.

12. Classifiers — ✗ Intentionally Excluded

Joylang does NOT use classifiers. Numbers attach directly to nouns: sanu satoombu (three friends), not "three [person-classifier] friend". This removes a major complexity barrier for non-East-Asian-language speakers.

13. Copula — ✓ melo — Always Present

The copula is melo (to be/exist). It behaves exactly like any other verb: takes tense, negation, and aspect. It is NEVER dropped.

Anu shishyombu melo. → I am a student.
Venu jovioku melo. → She is happy.
Oshi goloku vemelo. → That is not good.
Anu shishyombu melome. → I was a student.
Tenu guruvachi melobu. → You will be a teacher.

14. Case Markers — ✓ Seven Cases

Joylang has seven case suffixes that mark grammatical role. This means word order can be flexible without losing meaning.

CaseSuffixGrammatical RoleExample
Nominative-saSubject (who acts)Satoombu-sa kachilo. — The friend eats.
Accusative-koDirect object (what/whom)Anu satoombu-ko doshilo. — I see the friend.
Dative-raIndirect object (to/for)Anu tenu-ra dilo. — I give to you.
Genitive-niPossession (of/whose)Sato-ni mavombu — the friend's water
Locative-mePlace/time (in/at/on)Vidyombu-me nashilo. — studies at school
Ablative-taSource (from)Nagombu-ta belo. — comes from the city
Vocative-eDirect addressSato-e, namashte! — O friend, hello!
⚠ No Case on Adjectives or Adverbs Only nouns and noun phrases take cases. Adjectives and adverbs never take case suffixes.
✗ WRONG: Anu goloku-ko melo.    ✓ RIGHT: Anu goloku melo.

All universal question meanings are covered by seven dedicated words. They always appear after ka (the question particle) at the sentence start.

Question WordMeaningExample

16. Negation — ✓ ve- Prefix — Universal and Regular

Any verb can be negated by attaching ve- to its front. The prefix never changes, regardless of tense, aspect, or subject.

PositiveNegativeMeaning
kachilovekachiloeats / does not eat
melovemelois / is not
belovebelocomes / does not come
kachishilovekachishilois eating / is not eating
kachivimevekachivimehad eaten / had not eaten

Negative questions: ve- + verb inside a ka… sentence: Ka tenu vekachilo? = Don't you eat?

Emphatic negation: Add kadanu (never/absolutely not) after the verb for extra emphasis.

17. Demonstratives — ✓ Two-Way Near/Far Distinction

Joylang uses a 2-distance system (proximal/distal), not a 3-distance system. This is simpler and covers 95% of usage.

18. Evidentials — ✓ Four Evidentials — Optional

Evidentials come at the end of the sentence and are optional. Making evidentials optional (not mandatory) is right for an auxiliary language — mandatory evidentials would create high-stakes errors for beginners.

EvidentialSource of KnowledgeExample
diruI directly saw/experienced itVenu jovioku melo diru. — She is happy (I saw it myself).
shoruSomeone told me / hearsayVenu velo shoru. — He went, apparently.
niruI infer / it seems logicalTenu nashishilo niru. — You seem to be studying.
boruI read it / from a sourceJoylang goloku melo boru. — Joylang is good (I read it).
📌 Cultural Note Evidentials are one of the most culturally important features of Joylang. Using 'diru' vs 'shoru' vs 'niru' shows intellectual honesty — you are telling your listener exactly how reliable your information is. Joylang speakers value this transparency.

📌 Coverage Summary Joylang covers 16 of the 18 universal grammar categories. The two excluded categories — articles (determiners) and classifiers — are intentionally omitted. Articles are excluded because they are absent from most of Joylang's source languages (Hindi, Mandarin, Japanese, Swahili, Turkish). Classifiers are excluded because they are primarily an East Asian feature and would burden speakers of all other language families.
