Word Formation Rules

Morphology & Derivation — How every Joylang word is built from a root

Morphology Derivation

Design Philosophy

Every word-formation decision was made against three non-negotiable principles:

PrincipleMeaning in Practice
Easy to pronounceEvery derived word must be speakable by a global speaker on first attempt. Tier 2 sounds only. No illegal consonant clusters at morpheme boundaries.
Slightly funnySuffixes are chosen so derived words sound playful and unexpected — matching the Joylang personality (fun, warm, slightly silly).
Unrecognizable from rootA derived form should not obviously reveal its root. Learners must genuinely acquire derived vocabulary rather than guessing.

Each suffix choice is supported by cross-linguistic evidence, not personal preference. Every suffix and prefix was stress-tested across 10 roots from 5 language families (Indo-Aryan, Semitic, Sino-Tibetan, Japonic, Niger-Congo) before finalisation.

Root Word Architecture

Every root word is a neutral stem — it carries meaning but no inherent word class. The class (noun, verb, adjective) is assigned by attaching a class suffix.

Root Citation Form CVCV (preferred) or CVCVCV (3-syllable, for complex/abstract concepts)
Stem = root minus its final vowel: mavi → mav- | shaju → shaj- | pani → pan-
Class suffix replaces the final vowel: mavi → mav + ombu = mavombu (noun)

Root Length

LengthSyllablesWhen to UseExample Roots
Standard2 (CVCV)All common, everyday conceptsmavi (water), sato (friend), hape (wind)
Extended3 (CVCVCV)Abstract, technical, or complex conceptsnakameli, yashoburi

Two-syllable roots are strongly preferred. Three-syllable roots should be used sparingly and only when no clean two-syllable blend exists.

Phonotactic Constraints on Roots

  • Allowed syllable shapes: CV, CVC (word-final, soft consonants n m s l r ng only), CVCV (standard), limited CCV (stop + liquid: pr, br, kr, pl, kl, tr, dr)
  • Tier 2 sounds only: p b t d k g m n ng f s l r y v h ch j sh
  • Stress falls on the penultimate syllable — fixed, no exceptions
  • Syllable-timed rhythm — every syllable takes roughly equal duration

Root Sourcing Process

Roots use a mixed strategy: cross-language phonetic blending combined with phonesthetics (sound symbolism). The five-step process below must be followed for every new root.

StepActionExample — creating a root for "water"
1 — SurveyFind how the concept sounds in 5–6 target languages: Hindi, Arabic, Mandarin, Japanese, Swahili, and one other.Hindi: pāni | Arabic: mā' | Mandarin: shuǐ | Japanese: mizu | Swahili: maji
2 — ExtractIdentify the most recurring sounds and vowel shapes across the list.Recurring: m (Arabic, Japanese, Swahili), dominant vowels a and i, nasals throughout
3 — BlendCombine the most common phonemes into a Tier 2-compatible CVCV shape.m + a + v + i = mavi (v added for flowing-water phonesthetics)
4 — Phonesthetics checkDoes this sound feel right? Apply cross-linguistic sound symbolism.mavi — v is a fricative, feels like flow and movement. ✓
5 — Collision checkConfirm the root does not sound like any existing Joylang root, suffix, or particle.mavi is clear of all existing vocabulary. ✓

Word Classes — Class Suffix System

Roots are class-neutral. Attaching a class suffix signals how the word is being used grammatically. The suffix replaces the root's final vowel (stem extraction rule).

Rule Class suffix = drop root's final vowel + attach suffix
mavi → stem: mav- → mav + ombu = mavombu (a water / water-thing, noun)
-ombu
Noun class
mav + ombu = mavombu

Back vowel o signals concrete, tangible referents. Nasal-stop cluster -mb- is universally easy to pronounce (found in Bantu, common in natural speech). Final u closes it with weight.

-uvu
Verb class (dictionary form)
mav + uvu = mavuvu

Fricative v carries cross-linguistic action energy. The u-v-u mirror pattern is rare and highly memorable. Completely distinct from -ombu.

-oku
Adjective class
mav + oku = mavoku

Velar stop k is the sharpest, most categorising consonant — it pins down a quality. Short, punchy, unmistakable.

Derivational Suffixes

Derivational suffixes create new words from existing roots. They attach to the full root (keeping its final vowel), not the bare stem. The phonological boundary rules in the Boundary Rules section apply.

-vachi
Agent noun — one who does
mavi+vachi = mavivachi
sato+vachi = satovachi

Fricative v + affricate ch + front vowel i. Agent suffixes cross-linguistically use dynamic fricatives. Front vowel i links to animate/human referents.

mavivachi = swimmer, water-carrier
satovachi = one who makes friends

-chimu
Abstract noun — the concept/quality
mavi+chimu = mavichimu
sato+chimu = satochimu

Affricate ch creates a conceptual cut away from the concrete thing. Nasal final -mu gives weight — nasal endings appear in abstract/philosophical terms across unrelated languages.

mavichimu = hydrology, the concept of water
satochimu = friendship as a concept

-jiku
Adjective from noun — relating to / -like
mavi+jiku = mavijiku
sato+jiku = satojiku

Affricate j onset creates distance from the source noun. Velar k + u ending is crisp and relational — analogous to -ic, -al, -ish (English) and -ique (French).

mavijiku = aquatic, water-related
satojiku = friendly, friend-like

-ichu
Diminutive — small / cute
mavi+ichu → mavichu
sato+ichu = satichu

Front high vowel i cross-linguistically signals smallness (Sapir 1929, Ultan 1978). Affricate ch adds playfulness. The word literally sounds small and cute when spoken.

mavichu = a tiny drop (i+i elision applies)
satichu = a little friend, a buddy

-ongu
Augmentative — big / intense
mavi+ongu = maviongu
sato+ongu → satongu

Direct opposite of -ichu by sound symbolism. Back vowel o + velar nasal ng (deepest, most resonant nasal) + back vowel u = maximum acoustic weight. Physically lowers the voice when spoken.

maviongu = a flood, massive body of water
satongu = a great friend (o+o elision)

Stacking Derivational Suffixes

Derivational suffixes can be stacked, but only one level deep in standard Joylang. Two stacked derivational suffixes is Tier 3 (advanced/poetic) usage.

FormAnalysisMeaning
mavichimujikumavi + chimu + jikurelating to the philosophy/concept of water
satovachiongusato + vachi + ongua great friend-maker, a very sociable person

Negation Prefix — ve-

The negation prefix ve- attaches to the beginning of the verb class form (-uvu form). It is the only productive prefix in standard Joylang.

Rule ve- + verb root + tense = negated verb
mavuvu (to flow) → ve + kachilo = vekachilo (does not eat)
PositiveNegatedMeaning
mavuvuvemavuvudoes not flow
satuvuvesatuvudoes not befriend
kachilovekachilodoes not eat (present)
kachimevekachimedid not eat (past)

Why ve-?

  • Short — one syllable, minimal phonological weight added
  • Phonologically distinct — v is a fricative, no other suffix or prefix begins with v
  • Zero conflicts — does not clash with any case, tense, aspect, plural, question, or evidential marker
  • Slightly funny — vemavuvu, vesatuvu have a buzzy, defiant quality appropriate for negation

Negating Non-Verb Forms

Negation applies to verb forms only. To express "not X" about a noun or adjective, convert the concept to its verb form first, then negate:

Intended MeaningCorrect FormGloss
not wateryvemavuvu / vemavoku melodoes not have water-like quality / is not watery
friendless / no friendsvesatuvuone who does not befriend

Compound Word Formation

Order — Head-Last (Modifier First)

Compound words follow SOV logic: the modifier comes first, the head concept last. The head is the main meaning; the modifier narrows or qualifies it.

Modifier (first)Head (last)Compound FormMeaning
mavi (water)yasha (mountain)maviyashaa mountain with a waterfall / water-mountain
sato (friend)naka (river)satonakaa river of friendship (poetic)
naho (fire)shimi (tree)nahoshimifire-tree (a tree associated with fire)

Phonotactic Rules at Compound Boundary

SituationRuleCorrect Form
Root 1 ends in vowel + Root 2 starts with consonantJoin directly — no change neededmavi + yasha = maviyasha ✓
Root 1 ends in vowel + Root 2 starts with SAME vowelSame-vowel elision: drop Root 1's final vowelmoto + ombu → mot + ombu = motombu ✓
Root 1 ends in vowel + Root 2 starts with DIFFERENT vowelKeep both — glide resolves naturally in speechshaju + ongu = shajuongu ✓

Stress in Compounds

A compound is treated as a single phonological word. The standard penultimate stress rule applies to the full compound:

mavi-yasha → ma-vi-YA-sha

Partial Reduplication

Repeating the first syllable (CV) of a root as a prefix signals habitual action, ongoing repetition, or intensification of a quality.

Rule Copy the first CV syllable of the root → prepend it as a prefix.
For digraph-starting roots (sh, ch), take the first 3 characters: shoju → sho + shoju = shoshoju

Tier 1 Feature Reduplication is the only word-formation process that attaches before the root. It is Tier 1 (standard) usage and requires no special marking in writing.

Phonological Rules at Morpheme Boundaries

Three rules govern what happens when roots meet suffixes. These rules are standard phonological phenomena found in Swahili, Finnish, Japanese, and Turkish.

Rule 1 — Same-Vowel Elision (Active Rule)

When a root's final vowel is identical to the first vowel of the suffix, drop the root's final vowel before attaching.

RootSuffixRaw (incorrect)CorrectedRule Triggered
pani (ends in i)-ichu (starts with i)paniichupanichui + i → drop root i
moto (ends in o)-ongu (starts with o)motoongumotonguo + o → drop root o
sato (ends in o)-ongu (starts with o)satoongusatonguo + o → drop root o
shimi (ends in i)-ichu (starts with i)shimiichushimichui + i → drop root i

Rule 2 — Cross-Vowel Hiatus (No Correction Needed)

When a root ends in one vowel and a suffix begins with a different vowel, keep both. The sequence resolves naturally as a glide in speech.

SequenceExampleSpoken Resolution
u + ishaju + ichu = shajuichusha-ju-i-chu → "ju" glides naturally into "i"
o + inaho + ichu = nahoichuna-ho-i-chu → "ho" glides naturally into "i"
a + oyasha + ongu = yashaonguya-sha-on-gu → clean transition, no issue
u + oyolu + ongu = yoluonguyo-lu-on-gu → smooth, no issue

Rule 3 — Geminate Consonant Safety (Rarely Triggered)

If a root's stem-final consonant is identical to a suffix's opening consonant, insert -o- as a linking vowel.

Note This rule was not triggered once across 90 test derivations (10 roots × 9 affixes). It exists as a safety net for future vocabulary. When in doubt, insert -o-.

Loanword Adaptation

Loanwords are borrowed only when no clean blended root can be invented. The priority is always to create a new root first. When borrowing, adapt — do not copy directly.

StepRuleExample: English "sport"
1 — Replace non-Tier-2 soundsSwap any sound outside Tier 2 with the closest Tier 2 equivalent.sport → sipor (sp-cluster broken by inserting vowel i)
2 — Adapt syllable shapeGet as close to CVCV as possible. CVC word-endings are allowed.sipor → siporu (final vowel added)
3 — Add class suffixTreat as a regular Joylang root and attach the appropriate class suffix.sipor + ombu = siporombu (sport as a noun)
4 — Proper nouns exemptNames of people and places need no class suffix. Vocative -e applies normally.London → Londone (calling), Londonsa (subject)
